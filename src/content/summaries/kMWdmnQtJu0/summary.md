## Key Takeaways

Designers should move from being passive order-takers to active navigators of ambiguity by utilizing structured frameworks for problem-solving.

* Identify **eigenquestions** (or tipping point questions) at the start of a project to determine which decisions will make all subsequent choices obvious.

* Map design explorations on a **spectrum** between two polar extremes to help stakeholders understand the mental models and trade-offs behind each direction.

* Always present a **clear recommendation** or a "straw man" option to force a reaction, rather than offering a menu of choices that shifts the decision-making burden onto others.

* Use **high-fidelity prototypes** early in the process as a "flashlight" to get realistic feedback, while using the disclaimer "it could look like this" to maintain flexibility.

### Designing to Discover
A common mistake is waiting for perfect clarity before starting to design. Instead, designers should use the act of designing as a method for finding the answer. Michael Wandeler highlights the importance of the **"ridiculously early hypothesis."** By coming back to stakeholders just one week after a kickoff with a structured narrative and a specific point of view, designers can generate immediate momentum. This forces the team to react to a concrete idea rather than debating abstract concepts for weeks.

### Communication Tactics
Finally, the video emphasizes the importance of storytelling and "wiggle room."
